Biog

Midfielder Pat McGinlay came to Celtic in July 1993 from Hibs for a fee which would eventually be set by tribunal at £525,000.

Partick-born McGinlay’s move to Parkhead was a dream come true for the player who had earned a reputation at Easter Road as a midfielder who loved to get forward and score goals. Hoped to give our side some steel in the side, and hoped to cover for any cases where there were injuries to our main midfield maestros, McStay & Collins.

His good form with the Edinburgh side had seen him linked in the press to moves south of the border with Tottenham supposedly keen on taking him to London before Celtic stepped in. However his transfer was more costly than the cash-strapped Celtic board had hoped – they had bid only £245,000 - and his purchase had to be funded by the sale of out of favour Joe Miller, Steve Fulton and Stuart Slater.

Pat McGinlay made his competitive Hoops debut as a sub in a 2-2 league draw at Motherwell on August 7th. McGinlay’s form with Hibs was not evident in his early Celtic appearances (partly as he was not playing through the middle at Celtic as he did at Hibs) and despite some excellent performances against Rangers he looked like a player struggling to settle into his new surroundings.

There was a change of manager from Liam Brady to Lou Macari, the change in their philosophies not being one that would suit McGinlay's style of play. Hapless Celtic manager Lou Macari appeared to be unsure of exactly where he wanted Pat McGinlay to play; Lou Macari was truly hopeless and never helped anything. Macari wasted the talent that McGinlay had with us and the value he could bring to the first team.

The support generally warmed to him as his work effort couldn't be faulted. However, the side was generally poor and he was playing in what was one of our worst seasons to date. He played above average to the others, and to highlight how poor the first team was, he was our top scorer (that's right, our midfielder scored more than any of our strikers!). As the statistics show, he wasn't even that prolific a scorer at Celtic but supporters when they look back on the period seem to think he scored more than he did.

It says much about how poor Celtic were during this period that despite never really convincing the management, McGinlay was still one of Celtic’s main goal threats (if not the only one at points). Some people have to note that he was unlikely to be the most lauded midfielder when Collins & McStay were in the side, so he had a mountain to climb, but just taking his performances on their own then it's correct to say he was a very good player in an ailing side. He was probably the hardest battling player at points when many others had their heads down.

However come the new season it was clear Pat McGinlay did not fit into the first-team plans of newly appointed Celtic manager Tommy Burns who splashed out to bring in Phil O’Donnell from Motherwell. McGinlay was viewed as surplus to requirements and in November 1994 he returned to Hibernian for £420,000.

Sections of the support were unhappy he was moving on, but with the new management in, it was likely the best move for him if the new management wanted a fresh start and squad. McGinlay had a good start to the 1994-95 season as well, especially in the 2-0 victory over Rangers at Ibrox on Aug 27th, so possibly the transfer was premature.

The cash raised from selling McGinlay helped Tommy Burns buy Tosh McKinlay from Hearts. Coincidentally, Tosh was one of Pat's old St Peters Boys School teammates.

His tenure was round the days of the Celtic Takeover, and Celtic on the field were a shamble. Off the field was heavily impacting the players, and even once the takeover had occurred, the club was still cash-strapped with the need for planning for a new stadium and refreshing of a dilapidated squad.

He never got a chance to play for the senior Scotland side, but he was also unfortunate to miss out on the Ireland adventure in the World Cup in 1994 (USA). McGinlay was watched by manager Jack Charlton but not picked and given a chance. Maybe if Celtic had been in better order then he'd have been given an opportunity but it wasn't to be.

He had a good run in the first team and likely deserved a longer time with the club. He had the honour of scoring the final goal by a Celtic player at Celtic Park before it was renovated.

He remained at Easter Road for six seasons before moving to Ayr United. Whilst at Ayr, McGinlay enjoyed a cup run which ended with him being on the losing side in the 2002 Scottish League Cup Final, closer to silverware than ever he was at Celtic in those dark days.

At time of writing he has started a cleaning business and is doing very well from it. We hope him the best.

McGinlay move: Hibs tell Celtic they must wait in the wings

JIM REYNOLDS

Herald

Wednesday 16 June 1993
SPECULATION surrounding the future of Hibernian's 26-year-old midfield man Pat McGinlay was yesterday firmly put on hold by the player's agent, Bill McMurdo, despite reports that Celtic are preparing a bid of around #400,000 for the man who went to Easter Road on a free transfer from Blackpool six years ago. McGinlay, who has scored almost 30 goals from the midfield over the last two seasons, will be out of contract at the end of the month and has made no secret of the fact that if Celtic made a move for him, he would be most interested in hearing what they had to say.

I understand, however, that the Easter Road club has refused Celtic permission to speak to the player while he is still under contract, and matters look likely to stay that way until the month is out.

''We will not be speaking to any club about Pat's situation until July 1 at the earliest,'' said McMurdo. ''When his contract with Hibs expires, we will listen to all interested parties. At present, two English sides and one Scottish club have shown an interest.'' Despite the delay in signing talks, however, it is almost certain McGinlay will not be at Easter Road next season. And Celtic must be favourites to take him to Parkhead, despite the moves from England, believed to be from Nottingham Forest and Sunderland. Meanwhile, Celtic and CR Smith have teamed up again in a four-year shirt sponsorship deal which is worth just more than #1m to the premier division side.

Yesterday's announcement is expected to be followed up next week with news of the club's financial package, which they say will enable them to move to a new stadium in Cambuslang. That package will have to be more impressive than the one finalised yesterday, and until work starts on Celtic's dream home, outsiders remain entitled to be sceptical.

However, the optimism which was evident around Celtic Park last week when Liam Brady disclosed that Joe Jordan had agreed to become assistant manager, could be felt again as Celtic's chairman Kein Kelly said: ''CR Smith have provided valuable support in the past and their renewed commitment reflects the optimism both of us feel about the future.''

The chairman also pointed out that the sponsorship marks the launch of a new strip. The bands have been broadened and the collar design altered and although it will probably sell in the shops, the traditionalists might argue that the hoops are no longer the same.

Gerard Eadie, managing director of CR Smith, who withdrew from Celtic Park in 1991 after a seven-year stint, said he felt the time was right to become involved again. #400,000 for the man who went to Easter Road on a free transfer from Blackpool six years ago.
