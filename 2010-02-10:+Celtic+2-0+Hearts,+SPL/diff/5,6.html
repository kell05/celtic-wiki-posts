Loovens and Fortuné soothe Celtic's nerves in fevered arena

11 February 2010
Provided by: The Times

Celtic 2
Heart of Midlothian 0
This was a good night for Celtic, certainly in the ledger that is the Clydesdale Bank Premier League, if not always on the field of play. Tony Mowbray's new-fangled team exhibited their usual blips and gremlins but two goals early in the second half from Glenn Loovens and Marc-Antoine Fortuné were enough to see off Heart of Midlothian.
This, though, was just the half of it. Almost more important was the news from Fir Park, greeted with a jangling excitement around this stadium, that Rangers had stumbled to a 1-1 draw against Craig Brown's Motherwell. The night left Celtic eight points behind their arch-rivals — two better off than they had been at the start of the evening.
Given this turn of events, an edgy Celtic crowd could even forgive Robbie Keane for failing to score on his home debut, despite having at least three glaring chances to so do. If this had been Georgios Samaras or even Fortuné up to such waste, the crowd here would certainly have let them know about it. With Keane, though, and this implausible messiahstatus conferred on him at Celtic, there were but a few compassionate grumbles.
The Ireland striker has tricks aplenty, but the truth is, it is Diomansy Kamara who has made the most impressive of all these fresh career starts at Celtic in recent days. The Senegal striker has a ranginess that carries him effortlessly past opponents, and on this evidence you can see why Fulham, even if they were a bit carried away, paid £6.5 million for him two years ago. Kamara helped to soothe Celtic on a jittery night for Mowbray's players. Celtic are punting the "Keane effect" for all it is worth. Outside Celtic Park these days, right above the front porch, are two giant posters of the Irishman, with the ad-men urging supporters to come and taste the experience. The Celtic strategy is clearly to milk Keane's presence for all it is worth over the next five months. Oddly, though, the Celtic support was subdued, almost even apprehensive, last night. The attendance, while exceeding 50,000, was very decent, though not quite at the fit-to-burst extreme that many had predicted. And the anxiety around the stadium was tangible, with a nervous home support trying to get their minds around this brittle Celtic team. None of this atmosphere was helped by Celtic's awkward, error-strewn first half, when Mowbray's players approached the ball at times as if it were a parcel-bomb. Keane simply could not get into the game, partly a result of a Celtic midfield — Landry N'Guemo, Scott Brown and Kamara — that struggled to get any purchase on proceedings. The mood within Celtic Park was one of unease. Both Keane and Brown had shots beaten away by Marian Kello on the half hour — good chances both — and then Fortuné saw his effort deflected over Kello's bar. To the restless crowd this was Celtic at least posing some sort of threat, yet Hearts could still be satisfied at the way they harried their hosts out of their stride in the middle of the park. The Edinburgh side simply lapped up Celtic's endless sequence of misplaced passes. The first half ended on a sour note for Celtic — and with something of a lost temper by the referee, Brian Winter — when Aiden McGeady was booked for persistent complaining to the match official. McGeady was aggrieved at the way a foul had been given against him moments before the break and was still mouthing off to the referee — while booting the ball away — as the players left the pitch. Remarkably, Winter seemed to lose his rag with the players, wildly gesticulating himself at Celtic's players as they surrounded him in protest. It all looked half-daft, to be honest. By heavens, the opening minutes of the second half brought a blessed relief to Celtic. Having been so laboured, Mowbray's men suddenly found themselves two goals to the good before the 50th minute, thanks in part to some weird, slovenly Hearts defending when Loovens struck Celtic's opener after 49 minutes. From Kamara's corner the Dutch defender was allowed to cumbersomely gather the ball, and even miscontrol it, before still having space and time to shoot past Kello from 12 yards. You would be hard-pressed to say it was a goal Celtic deserved, yet within 60 seconds they would have another. This time it was much more inspired, Fortuné taking McGeady's delicately-slipped pass to roam into the Hearts area and then ram the ball low past Kello. In this brief, eventful minute the atmosphere in this arena was transformed, aided somewhat by the news of an ailing Rangers at Fir Park. Twice more Kello blocked Keane when he was clean through. But Celtic, mercifully for their supporters, held on for this much-covered win. All rights reserved The Times Celtic manager Tony Mowbray: "Hearts made it frustrating for us, yet we could have scored a couple in the first half. "Robbie Keane's a bit frustrated in the dressing room, but it will come for him. He's getting into fantastic areas and had a few one-on-ones with the keeper tonight, who made some good saves. "In the second half, once we scored early on it opened the game up a little bit and they had to try and push more men forward. That opened the door for us and on another night we could have scored quite a few. "I'd like to think Aiden McGeady is reaping the benefits of being on the bench for a game or two. Now he doesn't have to be the sole contributor to creative play because we have other footballers who can do that. I think we saw the benefits of a hungry and fresh McGeady. "Every point dropped by Rangers is important as we try to chip away at their lead, but we have to look after our own game." Hearts manager Jim Jefferies: "In the main I was happy at the first half in terms of the shape we had and the work the boys put in. "I told them at half-time that the important period was the next 10 or 15 minutes, but we lost a bad goal from a corner and it was virtually game over when we lost another on the back of that. "I can't fault the players for lack of effort, but there was a lack of options and better quality in many areas of the pitch. "I'm learning about the characters we have and who might be good enough to take the team to where I want it to be. "I'm hoping a new contract with Jose Goncalves can be sorted before Saturday's game."
