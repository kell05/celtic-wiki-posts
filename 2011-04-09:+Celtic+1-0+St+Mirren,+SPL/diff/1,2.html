Review

(Costabhoy of KDS forum)
Can't say too much as I didn't see the game but it's occurred to me that quite a few folk were asking for changes to be made for this, an apparently easy, game - firstly because some could probably do with a rest and secondly because we had four players who, if booked, would miss the huns game. I was certainly in agreement with the bookings argument but in hindsight (a wonderful thing) I'm now thinking that at this stage of the season, the next game is the biggest game of all. We're now five points clear (thank eff) and if the huns drop points tomorrow then we'll be in the position whereby so long as we match the huns in the games against the rest of the SPL we could afford to lose at Ibrox and still win the league. As such, every single game is must win and we need to focus 100% on that particular game, not take our eyes off the ball by worrying about what happens after it. If we hadn't won today the league would once again have been wide open but as it is, the pressure is back on the huns.

So... from now on, I'll be advocating our strongest side to start every single game not saying that maybe Wilson and Brown should be rested (as I did today in the pre-match thread) for whatever reason because of the game after next.

Just get each game won, one by one and with any luck, we'll be comfortably ahead by the time we play them.

Substitute Kris Commons spares sluggish Celtic's blushes with belated winner against St Mirren

Telegraph

Despite being strangely lethargic Celtic edged a nervy win to put pressure on Rangers, who are five points behind as they travel to meet Hamilton Academical in their game in hand. By Roddy Forsyth 9:33PM BST 09 Apr 2011

Although the final Old Firm derby of the season – at Ibrox on Easter Sunday – is still two weeks away, Celtic’s team selection was made with one eye on the collision with Rangers. Scott Brown, Mark Wilson and Georgios Samaras, each only one booking away from a suspension that would keep them out of the likely league decider, were omitted from the starting line-up.
This gave an opportunity to the two South Koreans, Cha Du Ri and Ki Sung Yeung, as well as Freddie Ljungberg, who got his first start in three months.
St Mirren, who had scored three goals in each of their two games in the past week – against Hamilton and Aberdeen – made five changes. Aaron Mooy, David van Zanten, Craig Dargo and Jure Travner were all rested, while Paul McGowan could not play against his principal employers.
Danny Lennon, the Saints manager, explained that the tempo he had demanded of his players was the prime reason for giving some of them a chance to recharge.
Having frequently been deployed with five at the back on their visits to the east end of Glasgow, they turned out on this occasion 4-1-4-1, a shape that worked so well for them in the first half that there were ominous grumbles from the Celtic support when the break arrived with the match still scoreless.

Celtic looked sluggish from the kick-off and the first sign that matters were not going as ordered was that Ljungberg and James Forrest were told to exchange positions on the flanks.

Even so, they could not make significant inroads and the best chance – indeed, almost the only decent chance – was headed wide by Anthony Stokes when he met a cross from Cha at the back post.

St Mirren, meanwhile, squandered two fine opportunities to take the lead, first when Steven Thomson kicked air in front of Fraser Forster’s goal and then, in the final minute of the half when the usually reliable Michael Higdon – who netted a hat-trick against Hamilton – swiped wildly at the ball when it was delivered to him when he was unmarked on the penalty spot from Patrick Cregg’s corner kick.

With the burden of having to win to impose pressure on Rangers prior to this afternoon’s trip to New Douglas Park, Celtic had to freshen their approach and, sure enough, Shaun Maloney – who had been out for four and a half months because of an ankle injury – replaced Gary Hooper, victim of a knock close to the interval, for the restart.

Celtic were certainly more animated than they had been at any time in the first 45 minutes, but the breakthrough continued to elude them, to palpable anxiety in the stands.

The hour came and went with no progress, likewise the three-quarter way mark, by which time Celtic had made all three possible substitutions, with Ljungberg and Ki taken off in favour of Kris Commons and Joe Ledley.

St Mirren responded to the threat posed by Commons by bringing on Jure Travner for the tiring Kenny McLean on their left side. That, though, was where the damage was done when Celtic at last broke through the well organised cordon in front of Paul Gallacher.

It was Commons who reacted first when the ball ran loose on the edge of the Paisley penalty area and delivered a low shot, which had the power to sweep beyond the goalkeeper at his right hand post.

Even then, unaccountable slackness in the Celtic defence in injury time allowed John Potter to take the ball on his chest from a corner kick and then strike on the volley inside the box.
However, Potter’s control let him down badly and his effort swung wildly wide of the mark to the obvious relief of Celtic’s supporters.

Elsewhere, Hearts celebrated 125 years at Tynecastle but Motherwell left the happier side after a goalless draw that guaranteed Stuart McCall’s players a top six finish. They thwarted Inverness Caledonian Thistle, who took the lead against Kilmarnock at Rugby Park only for Liam Kelly to equalise immediately.

At Tannadice goals from Johnny Russell and David Robertson for Dundee United saw off St Johnstone, while Aberdeen disappointed their home support when they lost 1-0 to Hibernian.

Celtic 1-0 St Mirren

By David McDaid

Celtic won narrowly to extended their lead at the top of the Scottish Premier League despite a below-par performance at home against St Mirren.

After a lacklustre first half the home side made three changes in an attempt to inject more cutting edge to their game in the second period.

It eventually paid off on 79 minutes when substitute Kris Commons fired in a low drive from 20 yards.

St Mirren's John Potter missed a good close-range chance in added time.

The win takes Celtic onto 73 points at the top of the SPL table, five ahead of Rangers, who are inactive until they travel to play their game in hand against Hamilton Accies on Sunday.

The hosts had come into the game off the back of a 3-1 midweek win over in-form Hibernian, but the performance in the opening 45 minutes was a far cry from that clinical display.

Manager Neil Lennon, serving the last game of his touchline ban, would have noted down just one real chance for his men.

That came after half-an-hour when Anthony Stokes rose to meet Cha Du-Ri's cross from the right-hand side, and the Irishman's header from seven yards went narrowly past the St Mirren post.
Other than that Celtic were sloppy in possession and struggled to get in behind a very well-organised Saints defence.

Beram Kayal, Celtic captain for the afternoon, and Ki Sung-Yeung in particular were guilty of wasteful passes.

And as the home crowd grew restless Ki and Charlie Mulgrew resorted to efforts from distance, which never troubled Paul Gallacher in the visitors' goal.

Instead, it was St Mirren, perhaps enjoying renewed confidence after winning their last two games, who looked more comfortable, and they had better chances to break the deadlock.

Michael Higdon watched a glancing header go across goal after 13 minutes, and then Steven Thompson had a glorious opportunity from Kenny McLean's cut-back.

But the Saints midfielder swung his foot and completely missed the ball from an excellent position just 10 yards from Fraser Forster's goal.

Shortly before the break, Celtic's James Forrest showed more urgency, taking the ball in from the right wing, and beating a couple of players before steering a shot past the far post.

But St Mirren had the last chance of the half, when Higdon was afforded acres of space at a corner, drifting away from his marker before badly scooping his shot over from around the penalty spot.
Shaun Maloney was given his first taste of action for more than four months after the break, with Gary Hooper making way having had a quiet game.

The home side began to show much more intent, putting St Mirren under some sustained pressure, but still they failed to really test Gallacher.

And although the league leaders made tracks down the wings, their crosses were often poor, and when Emilio Izaguirre burst through the middle from left-back his attempt to slip the ball through to Maloney was overhit and the move broke down like so many others.

With Ki and Ljungberg proving largely ineffectual, they were replaced by the more regular first XI pairing of Joe Ledley and Commons.

And the change paid off, as shortly afterwards Commons gave Celtic the goal which, until then, had looked unlikely for the hosts.

Maloney received the ball 18 yards out, dragged it across the box before laying it off to Commons who rattled a first-time shot low inside the post from around 20 yards.

As the game drew to a close, St Mirren's Potter was inches away from a spectacular equaliser with a rasping shot from 25 yards which flew just over the bar.

And the Saints defender had a wonderful chance to level in added time, but he lashed a left-foot volley wide from just seven yards after Celtic had failed to clear a corner.
